# Tea Beyond the Mainland: Hong Kong, Taiwan, and Macao

When people talk about Chinese tea, the image is always the same: Wuyi mountains, a gaiwan, steam rising from a cup. But three places — Hong Kong, Taiwan, and Macao — developed their own tea traditions entirely apart from the mainland. Colonial history, mountain isolation, and maritime trade routes each shaped something distinct. One of them literally gave the word "tea" to half the world.

## Hong Kong: Tea for the Working Class

In the 1940s, British colonial officials took afternoon tea in expensive hotels — inaccessible to locals. Street stall owners adapted the recipe: strong Ceylon black tea, evaporated milk, filtered through a cloth bag. The cloth stained dark over time, the colour of nylon stockings — hence the name sīwà nǎichá (絲襪奶茶), "silk stocking milk tea."

The invention is credited to Lam Muk-ho of Lán Fāng Yuán (蘭芳園), who began making it in 1952. Hongkongers now drink 2.5 million glasses a day. In 2017, the technique was registered as intangible cultural heritage.

The other tradition is yǐn chá (飲茶), "drinking tea" — the morning gathering in a tea hall (茶樓 chá lóu) over dim sum and pu-erh or roasted oolong. Tea here is social backdrop, not meditation. Sunday yǐn chá remains one of the city's central family rituals.

## Taiwan: When Mountains Improve Tea

In the 1980s, Taiwanese tea farmers began cultivating slopes above 1000 metres — previously considered too steep and landslide-prone. The result: cold air, constant mist, and slow growth concentrate flavour in ways lowland cultivation cannot replicate.

The best-known region is Ālǐ Shān (阿里山), around 1200 metres: light, floral, with a long milky finish. Higher up, Lí Shān (梨山, "Pear Mountain") reaches 1800–3000 metres, producing two harvests a year with more intensity. Cultivar Jīnxuān (金萱, "Golden Lily") gives a creamy mouthfeel; Qīngxīn (青心) is bolder and more fruited.

Taiwanese tea culture also developed its own aesthetics. In the 1970s–80s, a movement called chá yì (茶藝) — "tea arts" — emerged: deliberate gestures, posture, and movement became part of the practice. Competitions judge body form (měizī měiyí, 美姿美仪) alongside the brew. One physical marker separates Taiwan's gongfu cha from mainland style: the gōngdào bēi (公道杯), "fairness pitcher" — each steep is decanted into it before pouring, so every guest receives the same concentration.

## Macao: Where the Word "Tea" Entered Europe

Step back to the 16th century. When Portuguese traders settled in Macao in the 1550s, they heard the Cantonese word *chá* and carried it home. Through Portugal it spread across Europe and onward — into Russian, Persian, Turkish, Hindi. That is why Russian says *chay* while English says *tea*: English got the word from a Fujianese dialect via Dutch sailors; Russian got it from Portuguese via Macao.

In 1662, Portuguese princess Catarina de Bragança married England's King Charles II and brought a chest of tea with her — introducing the drink to the British court, which then carried it across the empire. Macao was the relay point.

Today Macao's tea culture is quietly layered: morning yǐn chá in traditional halls, Hong Kong-style milk tea, and café culture with Portuguese overtones — pastéis de nata alongside strong black tea.

## Three Paths from One Leaf

Hong Kong made tea a street drink. Taiwan took it to mountain altitude and built a new aesthetic around it. Macao sent it to Europe — and gave us the word for it.
